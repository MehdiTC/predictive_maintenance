also generated and logged as an MLflow artifact during tracked training; both describe the same
registered version. All figures below are regenerated by `make train` from the checksummed feature
layer and read from `data/models/cmapss/FD001/reports/`.

## Intended use and out-of-scope use

**Intended.** Reproducible FD001 research and education, offline pipeline demonstration, and driving
this project's simulated online replay, monitoring, and maintenance-policy scenarios.

**Out of scope.** Safety-critical maintenance decisions, real equipment control, any claim about the
physical meaning of the anonymous sensors, and any claim of industrial deployment. The model is not
trained on proprietary industrial data and has not been deployed in any proprietary industrial
system. It has only ever seen simulated NASA data.

## How the champion was selected

Selection used the validation split only. All four families were trained over uncapped and
capped-125 targets on identical asset-level splits. Candidates first had to pass validation
operational gates (critical-alert recall and false-alarm rate); the lowest common-domain RMSE then
defined a 2% tolerance band, and the least-complex candidate in that band won, with NASA score, MAE,
latency, artifact size, and a stable candidate ID as deterministic tie-breakers. No replay,
calibration, or official-test figure can influence selection. The champion's recorded rationale:

> Eligible on validation critical recall and false-alarm rate. Its common-domain RMSE is within
> 2.0% of the best eligible RMSE; among that set it has the lowest declared complexity rank, with
> NASA score, MAE, latency, size, and candidate ID used as deterministic tie-breakers.

That a regularized linear model wins over gradient-boosted trees on FD001 is a genuine, reproducible
result of the tie-break-to-simplicity policy — the champion is chosen by explicit criteria, not
hard-coded.

## Accuracy metrics

RUL error metrics by dataset role (lower is better; NASA score penalizes late predictions —
overestimated RUL — more steeply than early ones):

| Role | MAE | RMSE | NASA score |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Validation (selection) | 10.62 | 13.83 | 8852.43 |
| Replay (held-out engines) | 10.54 | 13.96 | 5966.23 |
| Official NASA final-row benchmark | 11.69 | 14.41 | 309.23 |

The three roles are not directly comparable: validation and replay score every cycle of each
trajectory, while the official benchmark scores one late-life row per test engine (hence the much
smaller absolute NASA sum over 100 rows).

## Uncertainty (conformal intervals)

Split-conformal intervals were calibrated on the 5 calibration engines at nominal 90% coverage. On
the held-out replay engines, empirical interval coverage was **0.898** with mean width **39.15
cycles**. Because cycles within one engine are temporally dependent, this is a useful empirical
approximation, not a strict trajectory-level exchangeability guarantee.

## Maintenance-alert behavior

Alerts fire when predicted RUL falls to or below a horizon: **critical ≤ 30 cycles**, **warning ≤ 50
cycles**. On the replay engines the champion reached critical-alert recall of about 0.77 with a
false-alarm rate under 1 per 1,000 observed cycles in the capped evaluation domain.

## Simulated maintenance-policy results

A predictive policy (intervene at the first critical alert with at least one cycle of lead) is
compared against a reactive policy (repair at failure). **All costs are normalized, hypothetical
units — not currency, not real industrial values, and not a claim of real savings.**

| Scenario | Predictive cost/engine | Reactive cost/engine | Change | Missed failures | Mean lead (cycles) |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Base | 4.28 | 12.00 | −64.3% | 0 | 26.1 |
| Lower failure cost | 4.28 | 8.00 | −46.5% | 0 | 26.1 |
| Higher failure cost | 4.28 | 18.00 | −76.2% | 0 | 26.1 |

The predictive advantage narrows as the assumed failure cost falls, which is the expected
sensitivity and the reason all three scenarios are reported rather than a single headline number.

## Delayed online performance (honest uncapped view)

When the champion is scored through the replay/monitoring path against realized **uncapped** RUL
(engine 9, 201 cycles), MAE was 26.12 and RMSE 32.49 with critical-alert recall 0.645. These are
worse than the capped replay metrics **by construction**: early-life rows have true RUL up to ~200
while the capped champion cannot predict above 125, so those rows inflate regression error and break
interval coverage. This is the honest operator-facing view and is documented in
[docs/replay.md](replay.md); it is not comparable to the capped-domain replay metrics above.

## Provenance and reproduction

The champion is fully traced by checksum. The training manifest records the Git SHA, dataset,
validation, split, and feature manifest SHA-256s, and checksums for every output.

```bash
uv run python scripts/train_models.py          # regenerate reports/artifacts under data/models/
uv run python scripts/train_models.py --track-with-mlflow   # + MLflow runs, registry, model card
make mlflow-verify                              # assert registry champion == local bundle (diff 0.0)
```

An unchanged rerun verifies every checksum and returns `already_trained`; tampered artifacts fail
rather than silently repair. Joblib artifacts use pickle semantics — load only trusted,
checksum-verified files.

## Limitations

See [docs/limitations.md](limitations.md) for the full list. In short: FD001 is simulated; sensors
are anonymous; online collection is simulated via trajectory replay; conformal coverage is empirical
under within-engine dependence; and no result here demonstrates real industrial performance.
